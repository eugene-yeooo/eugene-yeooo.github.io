Human Skills: Identity Values

Published 3 April 2025


Explain a situation where you have made an ethical decision. Discuss how you weighed up the values involved in that decision, the decision you made, and how you reflect on the decision now:

I caught my flatmate cheating on his partner, and later he was also caught snooping in my other flatmate’s phone looking at her nudes. A decision was made (collectively by our friend group, though I was an integral part of this decision) to inform his then-partner and excommunicate him from our group.

This was a tough decision, because we figured that his partner would break up with him (she did so immediately), and we were also aware that we were his only close friends - besides his family, he had no other social support systems at the time, we were all he had. I had a real concern for his mental state as a result of this, to the extent of whether he might contemplate suicide - I checked in on occasionally after to reduce the likelihood of this happening.

From a distance, this might seem like a harsh course of action, but understand that he had a myriad of other negative behaviours that we tolerated and tried to correct (e.g. being a compulsive liar, gaslighter, claiming to be knowledgeable about something when he actually had no idea - this is actually life threatening when we’re out rock climbing and he is managing rope systems and literally holding my life in his hands). What he finally did, arguably a form of sexual assault on my other flatmate, was just the last straw.

In the first instance, my value for second chances and redemption outweighed my perceived sense for loyalty and partnership (we were all young, and he swore he wouldn’t do it again/never did it again, though I found later that was a lie). In the second instance, my values for integrity, honesty, taking responsibility for your actions and just being a good person in not hurting others around you overshadowed my initial values in redemption and forgiveness, which led to cutting this person out. Frankly, I was putting value on my own self, removing a draining, energy-sapping, negative force from my life.

Reflecting on these events now, perhaps if I made the decision to remove him from my life sooner, the latter event would not have happened and everyone would be saved some grief. It’s hard to balance values that are good on their own, but might conflict with each other in a complex situation. In any case, I only learnt what I learnt by making these decisions the way I did, and there’s no time like the present to do the best that you know you can.


Describe how your culture and the people around you have influenced your values and identity:

I recognise that a big part of who I am is the sum of the people around me. I am surrounded by people who are passionate about what they do, adventurous, dedicated and persistent in their actions, kind, caring, supportive and generous towards others around them, conscious about their impact on the environment, curious about the world and excited to learn more about it. I am proud to have these people around me - they inspire me to embrace these values, apply myself, and be the best version that I can be.


Identify your strengths and how they will support you during your learning journey:

I am capable of trying hard, being persistent, not giving up and embracing the challenge when engaging at the right level. This trait will be very useful when faced with challenging problems in learning how to be a programmer and trying to get unblocked/unstuck.

I can recognise when my environment is suboptimal for focussing, and what factors are not conducive to being productive. I know what it’s like to be in flow state, and I like being in it. These strengths help with the flipped classroom approach, where I need to be self-driven and actively participate in my learning journey.

I am adventurous and curious - I am excited to learn and try new things (though this might sometimes conflict with the ‘just in time’ philosophy 👀)

I like streamlining my processes and being as efficient as possible, which aligns with the ‘refactoring’ aspect of problem-solving and thinking like a programmer.


Evaluate your limitations in terms of your learning and career development. How might these affect your learning journey?

At times I can be overly-competitive, which is detrimental in situations where I should be more supportive and collaborative instead.

When I am not engaging/engaged at an optimal level, I may be easily distracted and end up procrastinating instead. This is not ideal for the learning process, staying on-task and getting things done in a timely manner.

I am generally a supportive person and wouldn’t say that I am an apathetic person, but I recognise instances where I could have done more/better to empathise with people in particular situations (e.g. giving and receiving feedback), which may hinder effective collaboration. I look forward to developing my ability to communicate, cooperate and work amongst diverse perspectives in the Human Skills portion of this course.

There are times where I am a bit of a perfectionist and focus too much on tweaking the minor details that may or may not even make a difference. This is detrimental to getting things done in a timely manner. Hopefully timeboxing will help me be a more decisive and timely programmer.


Share an example of when you were trying to work productively with others, but there was resistance or tension. Discuss strategies you tried at the time, how effective they were, and your reflections on what other strategies you would try now and why:

I was recently travelling through South East Asia with a group of friends, and there were different ideas for what we wanted to do, varying enthusiasm for the places we were visiting, and some people were downright not coping well with the warmer climate and with the local food. There was resistance to certain group activities, and there was tension just from being in each other's company. Classic group travel dynamics right?

I balanced two strategies at the time. One was to try being as accommodating as possible and making sure everyone got to do exactly what they wanted. However, this was sometimes at the expense of what I wanted to do, and the resulting suppressed resentment was less than ideal. The other strategy was to just remove myself from the situation and recognise that not everything has to be a group activity. We just broke into smaller groups and each did what we really wanted to do. Love my friends to bits but lowkey this just meant not being in the company of people being a downer and buzz kill.

Reflecting on it, a better strategy was to set proper expectations at the very start of the trip (before even), and to go in with more of a plan as a group. If there was more of a discussion about what we each wanted to do and what was to be expected at the places we were visiting, then everyone would have been on the same page and could adjust for what we each wanted to get out of the trip from the very beginning.
