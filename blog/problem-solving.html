Tech Skills: Problem Solving

Published 17 April 2025



One time I was blocked on a simple problem:

The problem I was faced with was a JavaScript Kata exercise called whereAreYou. I had come up with a relatively simple solution that I was confident would work. However, it kept returning a failed test, and the error message made no sense to me. I spent a long time feeling very frustrated, trying to make little syntaxial adjustments in the hopes of passing the test by brute force trial and error, but with no success.

Eventually, I asked for help, and turns out the issue with my code was due to a simple misunderstanding of the different use cases for = and ===. Semantically in my head, I had the right idea for what I wanted to achieve, but I did not grasp the full logical requirement and the application of a conditional operator in this context. Upon reflection of this experience, while it is beneficial for critical thinking and solidifying my understanding to spend some time figuring out the right solution, there is a threshold where that is healthy or useful, and that I need to better recognise when I've crossed that line and need to ask for help.


One time I elegantly solved a problem:

Also in the JavaScript Kata, but with the deBee exercise this time - working through the prompts step by step, I had initially come up with a solution that passed the first 2 of 3 tests. However, the third test really stumped me, and I could not find a way to adapt my existing solution to accommodate the parameters of the third problem.

After a while, I decided to take a break from tinkering with my existing code, stepped outside to touch some grass, and then came back with a shifted mindset to search elsewhere for a solution. I remembered an instance from my JavaScript Intro exercise where I had simply misread what the question wanted from my solution (return at the end of a function instead of console.log), so now I decided to reread all the Kata prompts and also the Handbook. I noticed two things - the first was that each of the previous exercises included in their prompt to use a specific JS method, while deBee did not, and the second was that the Kata Handbook page listed one final method I hadn't used before. This was the .filter() method.

I did some research on this new method, and learnt that not only could it perform the same function as my previous solution, but much more. In the end, I was able to solve all 3 problems and pass all 3 tests with this new method. This experience highlighted how there are many ways of accomplishing a given goal, but some methods are more elegant than others, and we should always try to refine the way we problem-solve and refactor our solutions.



Different problem-solving techniques:

Here is a list of different problem-solving techniques, along with my own experience of implementing them.

  • Pseudocode: very useful for planning out and processing your solution step by step and wrapping your head around the problem at hand
  • Trying something: trial and error is useful for dealing with syntaxial errors, but less so for higher-level complexities
  • Rubber ducky method: haven't really done this for on-the-go problem solving, but I've found it useful for solidifying my understanding of a solution
  • Reading error messages: sometimes error messages basically hand you the solution, provided you already understand the concepts behind them, otherwise they may be too vague for you to do anything with them
  • Console.log: works very well to see the output of each unit of code you're writing, helps with debugging and making sure they return your expected output
  • Googling: the internet is an incredible resource for learning, usually my first stop for researching new concepts
  • Asking your peers for help: I've only done this a couple times, usually after I've exhausted all other problem-solving options
  • Asking coaches for help: also something I resort to after all other options fail to overcome my block
  • Improving your process with reflection: as per my anecdote above, very useful for shifting your perspective and tackling the problem from a different angle. Also complimentary to how I've described using the rubber ducky method above, to solidify understanding and identify opportunities for refactoring

A time I was reluctant to ask for help:

I was having trouble with using console.log properly - the code I had written in my .js file were not showing up in the DevTools console after loading the file in the browser. I tried many ways to troubleshoot it, including searching for a solution online, but to no avail. I felt like I was missing something very simple and fundamental, and this made me feel reluctant to ask for help in the fear of appearing stupid. However, I recognised this way of thinking to be textbook fixed mindset behaviour, and realised that to grow and move forwards positively, I needed to swallow my pride and embrace that it's okay to ask for help, even with things that appear simple.
